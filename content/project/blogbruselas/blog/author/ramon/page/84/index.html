Blog Bruselas en español

El blog-guía escrito por españoles en Bruselas para los hispanoparlantes que viven aquí y para los turistas que aprovechan los vuelos baratos para descubrir el chocolate, la cerveza, la Grand Place y tantas otras cosas buenas.

Blog Bruselas es

Un blog en español escrito en Bruselas por unos enamorados de la capital de Bélgica, corazón mágico de Europa. Una ciudad pequeña y grande, llena de gente, comida, eventos y rincones encantadores; para descubrir y disfrutar sin dejarse aguar la fiesta por el tiempo (no es tan malo).

Para quienes pasan por Bruselas, porque vienen de visita, de turismo o tienen la suerte de vivir aquí. Sí quieres conocer más que los hoteles en Bruselas, aprovecha los vuelos baratos y vive la ciudad.

Blog Bruselas es el bebé de Ramón Suárez, bruseleño convencido desde 2003.

    Spaniards en Bélgica

    Autor: Ramón Suárez

    Soy un español que tiene la suerte de llevar disfrutando Bruselas desde septiembre de 2003. Llegué para un trabajo temporal, como tantos, y acabé quedándome. Este blog comenzó como una aventura personal para ir transformándose poco a poco en una especie de diario o guía de Bruselas, abierta a otros autores y en la que tratamos de mostrar cosas que hacer y ver en esta pequeña gran ciudad, cada uno con nuestra visión personal. En los ratos ocupados soy un profesional del marketing y la estrategia de negocios en Internet. Como no, también he comenzado un blog profesional sobre temas de marketing y empresariado en Internet. Podéis encontrarme en Twitter con estas dos cuentas que muestran dos perfiles perfiles: @blogbruselas y @ramonsuarez. Sí tienes preguntas sobre Bruselas es mejor que las hagas en la página de Blog Bruselas en Facebook. Somos más de mil y tendrás muchas más personas que podrán ayudarte. Si quieres ponerte en contacto conmigo para colaborar con el blog, enviar información o hacerte esponsor de Blog Bruselas, manda un correo electrónico a blogbruselas arrobado en gmail con un puntito com.

    Hermanado con Enjuto Mojamuto

    No sé si mi router es fan de la muchachada, pero el caso es que me ha empujado a sentir un lazo de hermandad total con Enjuto Mojamuto. Como fan y adicto a internet ya me solidaricé con él en el peor día de su vida.

    Ahora me siento hermanado con él tras la muerte de mi router (o más bien el de Peter, mi vecino, con el que comparto la conexión a Internet). El sentimiento está además multiplicado: no solo sufrí la amarga experiencia de encontrarme sin Internet un día, sino que llevo desde el sábado por la noche teniendo que buscar wifis, o güifis, o uifis, o como se escriba según la RAE, de garito en garito. Es como el día en que South Park se quedó sin Internet.

    Duro, muy duro…

    Ciudadanos promotores


    Acabo de terminar de leerme el libro “Ciudadanos promotores: cuando la gente es el mensaje” y tengo que decir que me ha gustado. Recomiendo su lectura a todos aquellos que estén interesados en un libro de inicación al mundo de los blogs y de la democratización de la información gracias a Internet.

    Que gusto encontrar un libro interesante sobre un tema que me apasiona. Este es el primero de una gran remesa que acabo de recibir por correo, cuyo contenido es netamente sobre los medios sociales y el marketing.

    Estos son los subrayados que he guardado en mis notas.

    Citizen Marketers: When People Are the Message

    By Ben McConnell and Jackie Huba

    The story of how amateurs and professionals commingle to assume new forms of ownership in the companies, brands, products, and people they closely follow, disrupting the traditions of the existing cultural filters and promoters.

    Citizen marketers create what could be considered marketing and advertising content on behalf of people, brands, products, and organizations.

    Kinds of citizen marketers:

    • Filters are human wire services. They collect information and package it into a daily or near-daily stream of links, story summaries and observations.

    • Fanatics are true believers and evangelists. They may do filtering, but they love to analyze de daily or weekly progress of a brand, product, organization or person and prescribe courses of action. They are essentially volunteer coaches. Entertainment and food are their favorite subjects.

    • Facilitators are community creators, they are like the majors of online towns. Most of them are independent of the company or product, but a few have sought the company’s blessing or tacit approval.

    • Firecrackers are the one-hit wonders.

    The work of citizen marketers is typically defined by three commonalities:

    • Personal expression.

    • Amateur status.

    • Freely given.

    People are the message: when word of mouth is the most influential form of media on their decision making; when the excess of advertising creates demand for products to block them; when their intent is authentic; wen they have roots of credibility.

    Citizen marketers are transparent about their motivations and interests.

    The goal of many citizen marketers is to build a relationship with the company.

    An audience needn’t be large to be influential.

    Authenticity contributes to authority.

    With citizen-created content, people are the message because their role as publishers or broadcasters hoists them above the boundaries that one-way media communicators have erected around themselves. Citizen marketers and online-content creators with substantial audiences and dynamic authority rely upon and thus interact with their audiences. They work in the public piazzas where other people write, work, and play, too. They are not the balcony standers of one-way media, concealed behind walls of organizational privacy. Participation is their medium and their platform.

    Guiding principle (not absolute): 1% of users contribute and create content (content originators.) they contribute because it is fun (not €) => Hobbies are supposed to be fun. Contribution to “read-write” culture.

    Unimportance of the distribution of income, like in families=> not everybody in a family expects a fair share of income ( Gary S. Becker, The Economic Approach to Human Behavior.)

    “It’s the users that need the power to moderate, and giving each one of them a slice of the power is the best way to do it” (Kevin Rose, founder of Digg.)

    Forces of democratization work incessantly against the forces of control. As the forces of democratization gain strength, they continually compel companies to become more transparent, sometimes against their will.

    Mashup: a hybrid combination of two or more data sources that when combined, create a new product or improve an existing one.

    Ray Kurzweil: the early years of the 21st century are in the midst of a paradigm shift: “the time to adopt new paradigms is going down by half each decade.”

    The rise of the citizen marketer is being fueled by two distinct technological realities: speedy broadband connections and affordable digital tools.

    Everyone is a publisher, everyone is a broadcaster.

    Feeds: do not consume energy (note: servers do, and so do computers and the software running in the cloud); are free; are almost instantaneous; their subscribers are tangible assets.

    MySpace and YouTube democratized the tools of self expression.

    The six lessons of YouTube:

    1. It was designed and built with com
      munity as its founding principle.

    2. It made sharing video content extremely easy.

    3. It is loaded with statistics (views, comments, rating,…)

    4. It encourages its users to personalize their public profile pages.

    5. Its user interface is restrained and simple.

    6. Its search functionality consistently bested Google Video’s functionality.

    Google did beat YouTube in: making videos downloadable and providing video creators the means to sell their videos.

    The democratization of technology has leveled the playing field for entrepreneurs to create their own stand-alone networks.

    The playing field has been leveled for brands, companies, and nonprofit associations.

    Hall of fame (note: recognition, credit and ego reward.)

    Listen to your customers.

    If you do not have a passionate customer base, you’re not going to have much of a brand left in a few years.

    When communicating with companies: threats and juvenilia (note: speaking like a youngster or kid) are counterproductive. Be hones. Ensure you represent yourself and your community well. Write intelligent letters. Ensure flawless spelling. (Note: be polite.)

    Focus of internet marketers is often an attempt to transcend brand awareness into action by creating amateur networks of affiliation: grant those who seek it the ownership they creave.

    Productive leisure bridges our work lives and our home lives while validating our role as workers in a free and capitalistic market.

    Everyone knows that nothing is perfect, yet companies persist inn portraying their perfection. That’s why authenticity is magnetic, it is truth made real.

    Market helping behavior: the behavior of everyday people who help one another with decisions on what to buy and who to buy it from.

    Altruism and empathy are siblings.

    Good Samaritanism: because of their natures, some people cannot stand to see others grappling with problems or having a bad time.

    Whether it is nature or nurture, citizen marketers often feel the need to give something to others.

    Personal relevance drives citizen marketers because a product, brand, company, or person has lit their creative fuse. Blog or website is a vehicle for their conceptualization of self. Their individual icons of devotion provide meaning. It’s the beginning of affinity, a relationship that feeds their enduring involvement. Relevance of brand is attractive to others who feel a similar intuition.

    Common good: the world can be a better place and contribute toward that goal. The common good means contributing, cooperating, or improving the community.

    Sanctions against freeloaders, even if delivered by few people, improve the overall fortunes of a group.

    Status is the natural human driver of success. Market-helping behaviors reaffirm or improve one’s value to social networks, status improves our chances of making connections and finding success.

    As outwardly friendly people, citizen marketers are typically well connected. They have strong networks to which they attend.

    Efforts to affect culture, not reflect it.

    Companies should partner with the people they are trying to reach: bring your target onboard.

    Millenials: young persons born after 1982. Highly adept to digital tools. They tend to reject the “extreme” individualism of the Gen Xers who preceded them. They are the generation most likely to have the biggest impact on participatory culture.

    Give people control or they will probably find something else to do: start turning control over to them with a vote, a voice, and a connection.

    Four stages of successful meme replication:

    1. Assimilation.

    2. Retention.

    3. Expression.

    4. Transmission.

    A meme spreads, typically, because it matches the experiences of others and exposes a truth.

    You are your Google results.

    Astroturfer (note: splogger): someone who fakes enthusiasm for a company or a product because he or she is getting paid to do so.

    Create an “interestingness” algorithm (Flickr.)

    Voting is a democratized way of determining interest and ranking, while “interestingness is something of an ethnographic scout, automatically aggregating the normal behavior of people and applying a mathematical formula against it.

    “Interestingness relies more on implicit measures, things that people were doing through their normal course of behavior through the system” (Bradley Horowitz, Yahoo.)

    To democratize something is to make it more socially equitable or available to a broader array of interested people.

    By working with We, the citizens, companies are bound to reduce their risk of failure by embracing the principles of democracy and participating in established democratized forums or creating their own. They reduce risk by dristributing it more evenly.

    Freedom of speech is the underlying and fundamental principle. Restrictions on speech that prohibit or limit criticism of a brand, company, or product may be good for the company, but they are onerous and pointless for a community.

    A democratized forum that restricts or eliminates criticism sacrifices its credibility (not anarchy: action can be legally taken against libel and slander.)

    The three Cs of working with citizen marketers:

    1. Contests. The quickest way to experiment with citizen created content. (Note: Do not remove content after the contest: keep links alive!)

    2. Co-creation. When a company co-creates, it involves customers in a partnership to produce a product or service.

    3. Communities. Companies that create their own communities are democratizing their call centers. They distribute knowledge to a greater number of people, creating two assets: an expanded knowledge base and an identifiable database of citizen-owners.

    Three core components to understanding the dynamics of community:

    1. Consciousness of kind.

    2. Shared rituals and traditions.

    3. A sense of moral responsibility to the community.

    Community as a core competency.

    A blog provides up-front and immediate qualitative feedback. It’s a meme catcher.

    A network is only as strong as its members, and members are reluctant to help a clueless network organizer.

    The willingness of everyday people to get involved and participate will not replace traditional marketing, nor will it solve all existing marketing problems. It is a supplementer, a booster. It may spell the difference between profit and loss, recognition and obscurity.

    The natural intuitiveness of the human condition has a nose to sniff out a fake when we haven’t willingly surrendered our disbelief.

    Artifice is what social media combats. Social media is the antidote to campaign-based thinking.

    Loyalty is the real goal in the age of amateur culture and citizen marketers. The citizen marketers are demonstrating their loyalty by devoting their time and resources toward their hobby work.

    Citizen marketers demonstrate a belief in social altruism, driven by a belief system that the more a citizen marketer contributes to the greater good, the more valuable their contribution becomes.

    It is the open and transparent nature of shared production that enhances and illuminates what companies strive for but often misunderstand: loyalty.

    Loyalty is a medium to growth. Loyalty is not an incentive program.The reward is the experience itself.
